In the Oxford English Dictionary (OED), the definition of money exceeds five semicolons, the primary meaning of the word is given as: “Current coin; metal stamped into pieces of portable form as a medium of exchange and measure of value.” Difficulties with the descriptive definition of money forced economists to propose its functional definition. “Money, - said the British economist Sir Ralph Hawley, - but these concepts ... are defined primarily by the use or purpose they serve.” In other words, money, regardless of its physical or legal characteristics, can be anything that functions as a device for exchange and measurement of wealth. The point is that money is neither a productive good like technology nor a consumer good like food; instead, it is a means of expressing relationships between different goods.

Almost any object that people value, especially if it is scarce, may have served as a medium of exchange. In ancient times, such goods as salt, sugar, tobacco, oxen and slaves were used in this way.

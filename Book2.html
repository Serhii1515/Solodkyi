Standards: Monetary and medium of exchange used as money is called "monetary standard". Such standards are of two types, commodity (precious metal) and non-commodity (paper, or so-called “fiat money”). The term “fiat money” comes from the Latin fiat “let there be”, as in Fiat lux (“Let there be light!”) in the Latin Bible. Fiat money is paper converted into money by decree of the state. The term “gold standard” refers to fixing the value of a unit of currency by tying it to a specified amount (weight) of gold. From the Middle Ages until World War I, gold-based currency, usually gold coins, was the norm. Non-metallic money was little used in Europe until the end of the 18th century. In the United States, gold coins and gold certificates ceased to function as legal tender in 1934. From the mid-1930s, virtually all governments adopted a fiat standard for their money, freeing them from any obligation to return the coins or notes to their owners in gold or silver.
